Market Orders Explained: Types & When to Use Each

📅 Jan 15, 2025 ⏱️ 10 min read 👤 InvestWise Team

When you're ready to buy or sell stocks, how you place your order matters as much as what you're trading. The type of order you use determines when and at what price your trade executes. Understanding the different order types—and when to use each—can mean the difference between getting a good price and getting a nasty surprise.

This guide covers all the essential order types every investor should know.

The Four Basic Order Types

Most trading platforms offer four primary order types. Each serves a specific purpose and has distinct characteristics.

1. Market Orders

A market order is the simplest type of order. It instructs your broker to buy or sell immediately at the best available current price. Market orders execute quickly—they're the fastest way to get in or out of a position.

Characteristics:

  • Guaranteed execution: Will fill, assuming there's a market for the stock
  • No price guarantee: The execution price may differ from the quoted price, especially for volatile stocks
  • Best for: Liquid stocks when speed matters more than exact price

Example: You want to buy 100 shares of Apple. The current bid is $178.50 and ask is $178.52. You place a market order and expect execution near $178.50-$178.52. For a highly liquid stock like Apple, the difference will be minimal—perhaps a penny or two.

When NOT to use market orders:

  • Thinly traded stocks with wide bid-ask spreads
  • Very large orders that might move the market
  • During extreme volatility (market orders can execute at terrible prices)

2. Limit Orders

A limit order specifies the maximum price you'll pay to buy (or minimum price you'll accept to sell). The order only executes if the stock reaches your specified price or better.

Buy Limit Order: You'll pay your limit price or less. If the stock never drops to your price, the order won't execute.

Sell Limit Order: You'll receive your limit price or more. If the stock never rises to your price, the order won't execute.

Characteristics:

  • Price guarantee: You'll never pay more (or receive less) than your limit
  • No execution guarantee: The order may never fill if the price never reaches your level
  • Best for: When you care more about price than timing

Example: Apple is trading at $178.50, but you think it might dip to $175. You place a buy limit order at $175. If Apple drops to $175 or below, your order fills. If it stays above $175, nothing happens.

3. Stop Orders (Stop-Loss Orders)

A stop order becomes a market order when the stock hits your specified stop price. It's designed to limit losses or lock in profits once a certain threshold is breached.

Buy Stop Order: Placed above the current market price. When the stock rises to your stop price, it becomes a market order to buy.

Sell Stop Order: Placed below the current market price. When the stock falls to your stop price, it becomes a market order to sell.

Characteristics:

  • Execution is likely: Once triggered, the order becomes a market order
  • Price is not guaranteed: After triggering, execution may occur at any price
  • Best for: Risk management—protecting against large losses

Example: You own Apple at $170 and want to limit your loss to 10%. You place a sell stop at $153. If Apple falls to $153, your stop triggers and becomes a market order, likely executing near $153. You've limited your loss to approximately 10%.

4. Stop-Limit Orders

A stop-limit order combines a stop order with a limit order. When the stop price is hit, the order becomes a limit order rather than a market order—giving you price protection but no guarantee of execution.

Characteristics:

  • Dual price protection: Both the trigger and execution are controlled
  • Execution not guaranteed: If the stock gaps past your limit, the order may not fill
  • Best for: When you want to limit losses but avoid execution at undesirable prices

Example: You own Apple at $170 and want to sell if it drops, but you're unwilling to take less than $150. You place a stop-limit order with a stop at $153 and a limit at $150. If Apple drops to $153, your limit order activates at $150. If Apple gaps down to $140 overnight, your order won't execute—you'll stay in the position.

⚠️ Stop vs. Stop-Limit: Which to Use?

Use Stop Orders when: You're primarily concerned about execution and willing to accept the current market price after trigger.

Use Stop-Limit Orders when: You're concerned about getting a bad fill price and willing to risk not executing if the stock gaps.

Time-in-Force Options

When you place an order, you can specify how long it remains active:

Day Orders

The order expires at the end of the trading day if not executed. This is the default for most retail investors. Day orders are appropriate when you have a specific price in mind for today only.

Good-Til-Canceled (GTC)

The order remains active until you cancel it or it's executed. Most brokers limit GTC orders to 60-90 days. GTC is appropriate when you have a specific price target regardless of when it's reached.

Immediate-or-Cancel (IOC)

The order must execute immediately or it's canceled. Any portion that doesn't fill is canceled. IOC is used primarily by large institutional traders.

Fill-or-Kill (FOK)

The entire order must execute immediately in one transaction, or it's canceled entirely. Used for large orders where partial fills are unacceptable.

Advanced Order Types

Beyond basic orders, most platforms offer more sophisticated order types for specific situations.

Trailing Stop Orders

A trailing stop order sets a stop price at a fixed amount or percentage below (for sells) or above (for buys) the market price. As the price moves in your favor, the stop price follows at your specified distance. If the price reverses by your trail amount, the stop triggers.

Example: You bought Apple at $150 and want to protect profits while allowing upside. You place a trailing stop with 10% trail. Apple rises to $200—your stop is now at $180 ($200 - 10%). If Apple falls from $200 to $180, your stop triggers and sells. You've locked in a $30 gain while allowing the position to run.

One-Cancels-the-Other (OCO)

An OCO order pairs two conditional orders. When one executes, the other is automatically canceled. This is useful for setting both profit targets and stop losses simultaneously.

Example: You own Apple at $170. You place an OCO order with a sell limit at $200 (profit target) and a sell stop at $153 (stop loss). If Apple reaches $200, your profit target sells and the stop is canceled. If Apple drops to $153, your stop sells and the profit target is canceled.

Bracket Orders

Similar to OCO but includes a profit target, stop loss, and optional trailing stop. When the profit target or stop triggers, the opposite order is canceled. Many brokers offer bracket orders as a single ticket.

Conditional Orders

Some brokers offer conditional orders that trigger based on criteria other than price—such as time of day, volume, or movement of another security. These vary by platform.

Order Execution and the Role of Market Makers

Understanding how orders actually execute helps you avoid surprises.

The Order Flow

When you place a market order to buy, your broker sends it to an exchange. Market makers (specialists on the NYSE, market makers on NASDAQ) stand ready to buy or sell at quoted prices. Your order executes against the best available offer.

Bid-Ask Spread

The bid is the highest price buyers are willing to pay; the ask is the lowest price sellers will accept. The difference is the spread. For liquid stocks like Apple, the spread is just a penny or two. For illiquid stocks, spreads can be substantial.

Slippage

Slippage occurs when your execution price differs from your expected price. It's common with market orders in volatile stocks and for large orders. During fast markets, a stock might move several percent between when you see the quote and when your order executes.

Best Practices for Order Placement

Use Limit Orders for Most Trades

Unless you need immediate execution, limit orders give you price control and help you avoid paying more than necessary. For most investors, there's rarely a reason to accept market order execution risk.

Always Use Stop Losses

If you're holding individual stocks, stop losses (or stop-limit orders) are essential risk management tools. Even if you have a long-term horizon, unexpected events can devastate stock prices. A stop loss limits damage while allowing room for normal volatility.

Consider Position Size

Large orders can move markets, especially in less liquid stocks. If you need to buy or sell a large position, consider breaking it into smaller orders to minimize market impact.

Be Aware of Extended Hours

Orders placed during pre-market (4:00 AM - 9:30 AM ET) or after-hours (4:00 PM - 8:00 PM ET) execute against thinner liquidity with wider spreads. Market orders during extended hours can be particularly risky.

Check Your Order Before Confirming

Double-check the order type, price, quantity, and time-in-force before submitting. A misplaced decimal or wrong order type can be costly. Many platforms require confirmation clicks, but it's easy to click through without thinking.

Understanding Order Routing

When you place an order, your broker routes it to an exchange or market maker. The SEC requires brokers to disclose their routing practices and give you options. Most retail brokers offer payment for order flow, where they earn small fees by routing orders to certain market makers.

This doesn't necessarily mean worse execution, but it's worth understanding your broker's practices. Institutional investors and active traders often seek direct market access (DMA) to control order routing themselves.

📊 Order Type Summary

Market Order: Execute immediately at best price. Fast but no price control.
Limit Order: Execute only at your price or better. Patient entry/exit.
Stop Order: Becomes market order when triggered. Good execution, no price guarantee.
Stop-Limit Order: Becomes limit order when triggered. Price control but no execution guarantee.

Conclusion

Understanding order types is fundamental to successful trading and investing. The right order type can protect you from poor execution, limit losses, and help you capture profits systematically.

Key takeaways:

  • Use limit orders unless you need immediate execution
  • Always have an exit strategy—use stop losses
  • Consider stop-limit orders when price control matters
  • Use trailing stops to lock in profits while letting winners run
  • Double-check every order before submitting
